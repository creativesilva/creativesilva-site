Category 1 of 8
Camera Anatomy & Basics
Term 01
Manual Mode
A camera setting where you control aperture, shutter speed, and ISO yourself. Gives you full creative control.
Term 02
Viewfinder
The window you look through to compose your shot. Some are electronic (EVF), some are optical (OVF).
Term 03
Sensor
The chip inside the camera that captures light and turns it into a digital photo. Bigger sensor = better image quality.
Term 04
Histogram
A graph that shows how bright or dark your photo is. The left side is dark. The right side is bright.
Term 05
Metering
How the camera measures light to suggest exposure settings. Common modes: matrix, center-weighted, spot.
Term 06
Autofocus
The camera locks focus on your subject automatically. Faster than manual focus for moving subjects.
Category 2 of 8
The Exposure Triangle
Term 07
Exposure
How bright or dark your final photo is. Controlled by aperture, shutter speed, and ISO together.
Term 08
Aperture
The opening in the lens that lets light in. Measured in f-stops. Smaller number = wider opening = more light.
Term 09
Shutter Speed
How long the shutter stays open. Fast (1/1000) freezes motion. Slow (1/30) shows motion blur.
Term 10
ISO
How sensitive the sensor is to light. Low ISO (100) = clean image. High ISO (3200+) = brighter but grainier.
Term 11
F-Stop
The number that measures aperture, like f/2.8 or f/16. Lower f-stop = wider aperture = more light.
Term 12
Exposure Compensation
A quick way to make a photo brighter or darker without changing your manual settings.
Category 3 of 8
Lenses & Focal Length
Term 13
Focal Length
How "zoomed in" a lens is, measured in millimeters (mm). 24mm is wide. 200mm is telephoto.
Term 14
Wide Angle
A lens with a short focal length (under 35mm). Captures a lot of the scene. Great for landscapes.
Term 15
Telephoto
A lens with a long focal length (over 70mm). Brings far away subjects close. Great for sports and wildlife.
Term 16
Prime Lens
A lens with one fixed focal length. Cannot zoom. Usually sharper and lets in more light.
Term 17
Zoom Lens
A lens that can change focal lengths. Flexible. One lens covers many shots.
Term 18
Depth of Field
How much of the photo is in focus front to back. Shallow = background blurry. Deep = everything sharp.
Category 4 of 8
Composition
Term 19
Rule of Thirds
Split the frame into a 3x3 grid. Put your subject on one of the lines or where they cross.
Term 20
Leading Lines
Lines in the photo that guide the viewer’s eye toward the subject. Roads, fences, hallways.
Term 21
Framing
Using something in the foreground (like a doorway or tree branch) to frame the subject.
Term 22
Negative Space
The empty area around your subject. Used to give the subject room to breathe.
Term 23
Centered Composition
Putting your subject right in the middle of the frame. Bold and symmetrical.
Term 24
Aspect Ratio
The shape of the photo. 3:2 is standard DSLR. 1:1 is square (Instagram). 16:9 is wide.
Term 25
Visual Hierarchy
The order your eye moves through a photo. The most important thing should be seen first.
Term 26
Crop
Cutting parts of the photo out to improve the composition. Done in post or in-camera.
Category 5 of 8
Lighting
Term 27
Natural Light
Light from the sun or sky. Free, but you cannot control it. Changes through the day.
Term 28
Studio Lighting
Artificial lights you set up yourself. Strobes, continuous lights, modifiers. Full control.
Term 29
Key Light
The main light on your subject. Sets the overall brightness and direction of light.
Term 30
Fill Light
A second, softer light that fills in the shadows from the key light. Reduces contrast.
Term 31
Backlight
Light coming from behind the subject. Creates a glow, halo, or silhouette.
Term 32
Soft Light
Light from a big or diffused source. Creates gentle shadows. Flattering for portraits.
Term 33
Hard Light
Light from a small or undiffused source. Creates sharp shadows. Dramatic mood.
Category 6 of 8
Color & White Balance
Term 34
White Balance
Tells the camera what "white" looks like in your scene. Fixes orange or blue color casts.
Term 35
Color Temperature
Measured in Kelvin (K). Lower numbers are warm (orange). Higher numbers are cool (blue).
Term 36
Color Grading
Adjusting the colors in a photo to set a mood. Common in commercial and film work.
Term 37
Color Cast
When the whole photo has a slight color tint, like everything looks too blue or too orange.
Term 38
Saturation
How rich or strong the colors are. High saturation = vivid. Low saturation = muted.
Category 7 of 8
Post-Processing & Software
Term 39
Lightroom
Adobe’s software for editing, organizing, and exporting photos. The industry standard for editing.
Term 40
Photoshop
Adobe’s software for detailed photo editing, retouching, and compositing. More powerful than Lightroom for single photos.
Term 41
Develop Module
The part of Lightroom where you edit a single photo. Has sliders for exposure, color, and more.
Term 42
Retouching
Cleaning up small details: removing spots, smoothing skin, hiding distractions.
Term 43
Masking
Editing only part of a photo. Used to brighten a face or darken a sky without affecting the whole image.
Term 44
Noise Reduction
Smoothing out the grainy look from high ISO photos. Trade-off: too much makes the photo look soft.
Category 8 of 8
Workflow & Project Types
Term 45
RAW
A file format that saves all the data from the sensor. Bigger file, but lets you edit much more in post.
Term 46
JPEG
A compressed file format. Smaller file size. Good for sharing online. Less data to edit later.
Term 47
Contact Sheet
A page of small thumbnails of multiple photos. Used to review and pick favorites.
Term 48
Creative Brief
A written plan that tells you the goal, audience, and look of the shoot. Common for client work.
Term 49
Mood Board
A collection of reference images, colors, and ideas. Used to plan the look of a project before shooting.
Term 50
Commercial Photography
Photos made to sell a product, service, or brand. Done for paying clients.

Study Plan
1. Read each term out loud. Hear the word.
2. Write the ones you do not know into your notebook.
3. Note a quick real-world example next to each tough term.
4. Quiz yourself the night before. Then again in the morning.
On test day, your notebook is your only resource. Make the notes count.
