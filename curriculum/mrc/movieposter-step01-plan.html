Movie Poster  •  Step 1
Plan Your Poster
Pick your story and sketch the layout.
PICK YOUR CONCEPT / 01 / 06
Choose or Pitch
Use one of the three ideas from the overview as a starting point, or pitch your own creative concept to Mr. Silva for approval. Then write your logline: one sentence that says who the story is about, where it happens, and what is wrong.
KEEP IT PG-13
Get It Approved First
Your concept must be school-appropriate and approved by Mr. Silva before you shoot. Scary with mood and suspense, never with blood, gore, or weapons.
RESEARCH THE GENRE / 02 / 06
Study Scary Posters You Love
Start with the scary movies you love. Pull up their posters and really look. Then look up a few more from horror and thriller films you have heard about.
Hunt for what keeps showing up. Good scary posters repeat the tricks that work, so the same colors, fonts, and ideas come back again and again. Your job is to spot those patterns yourself. Spend real time here, good research leads to good design.
Look For 01
Colors
What colors keep coming back? Look across a few posters and find the pattern yourself.
Look For 02
Fonts
Are the titles sharp, rough, hand-drawn, or clean? How does the lettering make you feel?
Look For 03
Layout
Where do the title, the faces, and the credits sit? How is the empty space used?
Look For 04
Hero and Villain
How do they show the good guy and the threat? Notice where each one sits and how big they are.
Look For 05
Repeated Tricks
What shows up on poster after poster? Those repeats are what works in the genre. Find them.

SHARE WHAT YOU FIND
Borrow the Best, Make It Yours
Talk through what each of you noticed. You will see the same tricks come up a lot. Borrow the ones you like, then make your own choices. Your poster does not have to look like anyone else’s.
WRITE AND CAST / 03 / 06
Build Your Movie
Now decide the pieces of your movie. These choices give you the people, the words, and the look for your poster. Scroll through each one.
Decide 01
Protagonist
Who is the hero, the star we follow? Cast a friend to play them.
Decide 02
Antagonist
Who or what is the villain or monster? How will you show the threat?
Decide 03
Title
Name your movie. Short and creepy works best.
Decide 04
Tagline
Write one haunting line that hooks the viewer.
Decide 05
Subtitle
A short second line that adds detail or sets up the story.
Decide 06
Studio and Cast
Make up a production company name and pick two or three actor names for the credits.
Decide 07
Location and Mood
Pick your spot and the mood you want it to feel like. What colors fit your story?

SKETCH YOUR LAYOUT / 04 / 06
Plan the Poster
On paper, sketch a quick thumbnail of your poster. Use design hierarchy: plan where each piece goes and how big it is.
Show your protagonist as the main focus and your antagonist (villain or monster) as a clear threat. Then decide where the title, tagline, subtitle, production logo, and actor names go. That layout is your call: use what you learned from the real posters in your research.
Leave room for the text. Both characters and the title should read at a glance.
SCOUT YOUR LOCATION / 05 / 06
Scout Before You Shoot
Real photographers scout a location before the real shoot. Scouting means visiting a spot early to study the light, the angles, and the background, so shoot day runs smoothly.
Go out with just your camera, no flash or tripod yet, and take reference photos. You are not making finals, you are gathering notes to choose the best spot.
Scout 01
Walk the Spot
Visit your location ahead of time with just a camera. No tripod and no flash, travel light.
Scout 02
Shoot References
Take quick reference photos of a few spots and angles. These are notes, not your final shots.
Scout 03
Read the Light
Notice where the light falls and how the background looks. Look for the cleanest, strongest angle.
Scout 04
Decide on a Computer
Back in the lab, study your reference photos on the big screen and pick your best spot and angle.
Scout 05
Plan the Setup
Decide where you, your subject, and your flash will go before you haul all the gear out.

WHY SCOUT
A Scouted Shoot Is a Fast Shoot
When you already know your spot and your setup, shoot day is spent making great photos instead of figuring out where to stand. Then you bring out the flash and gear.
GET IT CHECKED / 06 / 06
Get Approved
Before you shoot, show Mr. Silva your concept, your cast and location, your scouting photos, and your layout sketch. This is a required checkpoint.
CHECKPOINT
Approval First, Shoot Second
Your concept must be approved so it stays PG-13 and school-safe. Get the green light, then bring out the camera and flash for Step 2.
