WHAT IS A DOUBLE EXPOSURE / 01 / 07
Two Photos, One Image
A double exposure blends two photos into a single image. The classic look is a portrait or silhouette filled with a second picture: a forest, a city, clouds, or water. It is one of the fastest ways to make something that looks like real art.
The secret is blending modes: a Photoshop setting that changes how one layer mixes with the layer underneath it. Today you will photograph a portrait, cut out your subject, and fuse it with a second image.

GET YOUR TWO IMAGES / 03 / 07
Shoot One, Source One
You need two images. The first one you must photograph yourself.
Image 1, the portrait: Photograph a portrait or headshot of a person. Shoot them against a solid, simple background like a plain wall, so you can cut them out cleanly later.
Image 2, the blend image: This is the picture that fills your subject. You can photograph it too, or download one free from Unsplash. Good choices: trees, mountains, a city skyline, fog, smoke, or water.
FREE IMAGES
Find One That Is Free
Search for a blend image on unsplash.com → or the web, like “forest” or “mountains.” Heads up: not every image is free to download. Some need an account or a paid plan, and search results often link to paid stock sites like Shutterstock or iStock. Search for free images, then browse and pick one that is entirely free and also works for your photo. On Unsplash, open the photo and click Download free, then bring it into Photoshop.
CUT OUT YOUR SUBJECT / 04 / 07
Mask With Select and Mask
To blend cleanly, separate your subject from their background. You know this workflow: Select > Select and Mask > Select Subject. Here is the quick refresher.
Cut Out 01
Open Select and Mask
With your portrait layer active, go to Select > Select and Mask to open the workspace.
Cut Out 02
Click Select Subject
At the top, click Select Subject. Photoshop finds the person automatically.
Cut Out 03
Refine the Edges
Run the Refine Edge brush along the hair and edges to clean up the selection.
Cut Out 04
Output to Layer Mask
Set Output To: Layer Mask and click OK. Your subject is cut out and the background is gone.
Cut Out 05
Why a Simple Background
This is why you shoot against a plain, solid background: the subject separates cleanly and masks in seconds.

BLEND WITH BLENDING MODES / 05 / 07
Fuse the Two Images
Place your blend image on top of your cut-out portrait, then change its blend mode to fuse them. Here is where to find blend modes and what the main ones do.
Blend 01
Find the Menu
In the Layers panel, click the dropdown that says Normal at the top. That is the blend mode menu for the selected layer.
Blend 02
What It Does
A blend mode changes how the top layer mixes with the layer below it, based on the light and dark pixels.
Blend 03
Screen
Screen drops the dark areas and keeps the bright ones. Great for a light texture over a darker portrait.
Blend 04
Lighten
Lighten keeps whichever pixels are brighter. Similar to Screen, a little punchier.
Blend 05
Multiply
Multiply drops the bright areas and keeps the dark ones. Great for dark trees over a lighter portrait.
Blend 06
Cycle and Compare
Move down the list and watch the live preview. Pick the mode that fuses your two images best.

EXPERIMENT
There Is No Wrong Answer
Cycle through the blend modes and watch the preview. Move and resize your blend image, lower the opacity, and add a layer mask to hide parts you do not want. Keep going until the two photos feel like one.
WORDS TO KNOW / 06 / 07
Double Exposure Vocabulary
Learn these six terms. You will use them through the whole exercise.
Vocabulary
Double Exposure
Two images blended into one. The look comes from old film cameras exposing a single frame twice.
Vocabulary
Blending Mode
A layer setting that controls how a layer mixes with the one below it.
Vocabulary
Layer Mask
A black-and-white attachment that hides or shows parts of a layer. Black hides, white shows.
Vocabulary
Select and Mask
The Photoshop workspace for making and refining a selection, especially around hair and edges.
Vocabulary
Opacity
How see-through a layer is, from 0% (invisible) to 100% (solid).
Vocabulary
Silhouette
A subject shown as a solid dark shape against a lighter background.

TURN IT IN / 07 / 07
Export and Submit
Make two or three different double exposures using different blend images or different blend modes. Experimenting is the point, so do not stop at one.
For each one, flatten it with Layer > Flatten Image and export a JPG with File > Save a Copy.
Submit your 2 to 3 final JPG files to this assignment in Canvas. Name them: FirstName LastInitial - Double Exposure 1.jpg, and so on.
