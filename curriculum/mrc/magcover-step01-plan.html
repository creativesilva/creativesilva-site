PICK YOUR TOPIC / 01 / 05
Topic and Person
Choose a topic that fits the CTE agriculture theme of our campus, and a real person to feature: a student, an administrator, or a teacher.
Pick someone you can photograph and talk to. Your topic and your person should tell the same story, like “Future Farmers,” “Built at the MRC,” or “Meet the Welder.”
TEAM TIME
Brainstorm Together
Work with your group to share topic ideas and plan who to interview. Slow down and talk it out, then each of you builds your own unique cover.
INTERVIEW YOUR SUBJECT / 02 / 05
Get the Words
Now interview your person. Their answers become the words on your cover: your title, your subtitle, and your breakout points. Write down quotes and real facts.
Ask 01
What do you do here?
Get their role and their everyday work in their own words.
Ask 02
Why does it matter?
Find the bigger story. This often becomes your main title.
Ask 03
What are you proud of?
A strong quote or fact, perfect for a breakout point.
Ask 04
What is next for you?
Where are they headed? Great for a teaser cover line.

SKETCH YOUR LAYOUT / 03 / 05
Plan the Cover
On paper, sketch a quick thumbnail of your cover. Plan where each piece goes:
Put the masthead at the top, block out your hero photo, mark where your title and breakout points go down one side, and box out spots for your two breakout images.
Leave room for the text. Do not plan to cover your subject’s face with the masthead or headlines.
SCOUT YOUR LOCATION / 04 / 05
Scout Before You Shoot
Real photographers scout a location before the real shoot. Scouting means visiting a spot early to study the light, the angles, and the background, so shoot day runs smoothly.
Go out with just your camera, no flash or tripod yet, and take reference photos. You are not making finals, you are gathering notes to choose the best spot.
Scout 01
Walk the Spot
Visit your location ahead of time with just a camera. No tripod and no flash, travel light.
Scout 02
Shoot References
Take quick reference photos of a few spots and angles. These are notes, not your final shots.
Scout 03
Read the Light
Notice where the light falls and how the background looks. Look for the cleanest, strongest angle.
Scout 04
Decide on a Computer
Back in the lab, study your reference photos on the big screen and pick your best spot and angle.
Scout 05
Plan the Setup
Decide where you, your subject, and your flash will go before you haul all the gear out.

WHY SCOUT
A Scouted Shoot Is a Fast Shoot
When you already know your spot and your setup, shoot day is spent making great photos instead of figuring out where to stand. Then you bring out the flash and gear.
GET IT CHECKED / 05 / 05
Bring It to Mr. Silva
Before you shoot, show Mr. Silva your person, your interview notes, your scouting photos, and your layout sketch. This is a quick checkpoint, no upload needed.
CHECKPOINT
Plan First, Shoot Second
A clear plan and good interview make a stronger cover. Get the green light, then bring out the camera and flash for Step 2.
