READ THE BRIEF / 02 / 05
Pull Your Words From Teddy’s Story
Read Teddy’s brief and pull out the words for your cover. Your main title comes from her honor and her story, your subtitle adds one detail, and your breakout points tease other campus stories.
Teddy
Winner, 2025/2026 Classified Employee of the Year
Teddy is the security officer at the Mark Richardson CTE Center and Ag Farm, but her real focus is people. She practices “relational security”: she learns every student’s name, greets them by it, and makes the whole campus feel seen and safe. Students know her not just as an officer, but as a trusted mentor.
She is also the campus photographer. Her photos of student grit and determination have appeared in school assemblies, school board reports, and local media, building real pride in the MRC.
Teddy goes far beyond her job description. She checks on the animals daily, feeds livestock on weekends, helps the Robotics team prepare late into the night, waters the campus flowers, and pulls weeds, all because she cares. She even became forklift certified to keep students safe in an active CTE environment.
Principal Dr. Paul Robinson calls her watchful and dedicated, and bus drivers, teachers, and students all trust her. She was named the 2025/2026 Classified Employee of the Year, and as her nomination put it, Teddy proves that kindness and heart are the most powerful tools for building a strong school community.

SKETCH YOUR LAYOUT / 03 / 05
Plan the Cover
On paper, sketch a quick thumbnail of your cover. Plan where each piece goes:
Put the masthead at the top, block out your hero photo of Teddy, mark where your main title (the biggest text, about her) and your smaller breakout points go down one side, and box out spots for your two breakout images.
Leave room for the text. Do not plan to cover Teddy’s face with the masthead or headlines.

SCOUT YOUR LOCATION / 04 / 05
Scout Before You Shoot
Real photographers scout a location before the real shoot. Scouting means visiting a spot early to study the light, the angles, and the background, so shoot day runs smoothly.
Go out with just your camera, no flash or tripod yet, and take reference photos. You are not making finals, you are gathering notes to choose the best spot.
Scout 01
Walk the Spot
Visit your location ahead of time with just a camera. No tripod and no flash, travel light.
Scout 02
Shoot References
Take quick reference photos of a few spots and angles. These are notes, not your final shots.
Scout 03
Read the Light
Notice where the light falls and how the background looks. Look for the cleanest, strongest angle.
Scout 04
Decide on a Computer
Back in the lab, study your reference photos on the big screen and pick your best spot and angle.
Scout 05
Plan the Setup
Decide where you, your subject, and your flash will go before you haul all the gear out.

WHY SCOUT
A Scouted Shoot Is a Fast Shoot
When you already know your spot and your setup, shoot day is spent making great photos instead of figuring out where to stand. Then you bring out the flash and gear.
