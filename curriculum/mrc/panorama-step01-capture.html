Capture Your RAW Images
Camera, tripod, and RAW files.
YOUR TWO PANORAMAS / 01 / 07
One on Campus, One at Home
You will make two panoramas for this project. You shoot one here on campus in class, with the camera on a tripod. You shoot the other one at home for homework, holding the Canon R50 in your hands.
Each panorama is 4 to 5 photos that overlap. Look for wide scenes with interesting textures, structures, or open space.
SET UP YOUR CAMERA / 02 / 07
Camera and Tripod Setup
Get your gear ready before you shoot the campus panorama. Scroll through each setup step.
Setup 01
Format the SD Card
Erase old photos first so you have room and start clean. Format the card in the camera menu.
Setup 02
Set Quality to RAW
In the menu, set image quality to RAW only, no JPEG. On our Canon cameras this makes .CR3 files.
Setup 03
Mount the Tripod
Put the camera on a tripod and level it. A steady, level camera is the key to a clean stitch.
Setup 04
Switch to AV Mode
Use AV (aperture priority). Pick your aperture: a low f-stop like f/4 blurs the background; a high f-stop like f/11 keeps more in focus.
Setup 05
Set Your Exposure
Check the light meter. The photo should not be too dark or too bright. Take a test shot and adjust.

SHOOT THE PANORAMA / 03 / 07
Pan Slow and Overlap
Now capture your photos. Move across the scene in even, overlapping steps.
Focus pro tip: if the camera will not lock focus, flip the AF / MF switch to MF (manual focus). That turns on the focus ring on the lens so you can twist it to set focus by hand. Switch it back to AF when you finish, for the next student.
Shoot 01
Take 4 to 5 Photos
Shoot 4 to 5 RAW photos for one panorama, moving across the scene from one side to the other.
Shoot 02
Pan Slow and Level
Turn slowly in a level, horizontal motion. Keep the camera flat the whole way across.
Shoot 03
Overlap Each Shot
Overlap each photo with the one before it by about a third so Photoshop can match them up. More on this rule next.
Shoot 04
Lock Focus (Optional)
For a blurry background, turn off autofocus after your first shot so focus stays the same. Turn autofocus back on when you finish, for the next student.
Shoot 05
Check Your Shots
Look at your photos on the back screen. If one is blurry or you missed the overlap, shoot the whole set again.

THE KEY RULE / 04 / 07
Overlap Is Everything
This is the most important rule in the whole project: your photos must overlap. Overlap means each photo shares part of the same view with the photo next to it.
Photoshop builds your panorama by matching the parts that repeat. If two photos share enough of the same details, it lines them up and blends them into one smooth image. If they do not share enough, the stitch breaks, leaves gaps, or looks crooked.
Overlap each photo with the one before it by about a third (30 to 40 percent). When you are not sure, overlap more, not less. Extra overlap is free insurance for a clean stitch.
PRO TIP
Pick a Marker
Find an object near the right edge of your photo, like a post or a tree. Put that same object near the left edge of your next photo. That guarantees enough overlap every time.

TAKE IT HOME / 07 / 07
Shoot One Without a Tripod
For homework, take the Canon R50 home and shoot your second panorama off campus, with no tripod. You will edit it next to your campus one in Step 2.
No tripod is harder, but these tricks keep your photos steady and even. Same rules apply: pan slowly, keep the camera level, and overlap a lot.
Tip 01
Use a Wide Lens
Zoom out wide if you can. A wide view is more forgiving and hides small wobbles between shots.
Tip 02
Brace Your Arms
Tuck your elbows tight against your sides and hold the camera with both hands. Your body becomes the tripod.
Tip 03
Turn at the Hips
Keep your feet planted and rotate your whole upper body from the hips. That makes a smooth, even sweep.
Tip 04
Try a Tabletop
No steady hands? Rest the camera on a flat surface like a table or a wall, and slide or turn it a little at a time in one direction.
Tip 05
Overlap Even More
Without a tripod your aim drifts, so overlap each shot by a third or more. Extra overlap saves a handheld stitch.

REMEMBER
Same Direction, Even Steps
Move in one direction only, in small even steps. Do not skip ahead or jump back, and keep your overlap high.
