THE MISSION / 01 / 06
Your First Photo Hunt
Today you grab a real camera and go hunting, not for animals, but for great shots. The Mark Richardson Center is full of them: livestock and crops, big machines and work trucks, rusted metal, rough wood, and an American flag.
You do not need to know every button. You just need to look closely and frame each shot with care. This is how every photographer starts: by learning to see.
HOW IT WORKS / 02 / 06
Hunt, Shoot, Check Off
You get a paper shot list with about 20 items. Some are things to find, like an American flag or a tractor. Some are composition challenges, like leading lines or texture.
Find each item, frame it well, take the shot, and check it off your list. Work with your group, but everyone takes their own photos.
THE GOAL
Quality Over Speed
It is not a race. One well-framed photo beats five quick snapshots. Slow down and look before you press the shutter.
YOUR CAMERA, MADE EASY / 03 / 06
Let the Camera Do the Work
Set the dial to AUTO. In auto mode the camera handles the focus, the light, and the color for you. Your only job is what to point it at and how to frame it.
Hold the camera with both hands and tuck your elbows into your body to stay steady. Press the shutter button halfway to lock focus, wait for the beep or the green box, then press the rest of the way to take the shot.
Blurry photo? Hold still, press halfway again, and let it focus before you shoot.
COMPOSITION BASICS / 04 / 06
How to Frame a Great Shot
Composition means how you arrange what is in your photo. These are the moves on your shot list. Learn them here, then go find them out there.
Composition
Leading Lines
Use a road, fence, or row of crops to pull your eye into the photo.
Composition
Rule of Thirds
Put your subject off to one side, not dead center. It feels more natural.
Composition
Framing
Shoot through a gate, window, or branches to frame your subject.
Composition
Texture
Get close to a rough surface so the viewer can almost feel it.
Composition
Pattern
Find shapes that repeat, like fence posts or stacked pipes.
Composition
Fill the Frame
Get close so your subject fills the whole photo, with no empty space.
Composition
Point of View
Shoot from down low or up high, not just from eye level.
Composition
Symmetry
Line up a balanced shot where both halves match.
Composition
Negative Space
Leave lots of empty space around a small subject to make it pop.

RULES OF THE HUNT / 05 / 06
Stay Safe, Shoot Smart
You will be outdoors around animals and heavy equipment. Safety comes first, every time.
Keep your distance from livestock and machinery. Never climb on or reach into equipment, and never enter a pen or a gated area. Watch your footing on uneven ground.
Stay with your group and your teacher. Respect the campus and the animals. Photograph the work, but do not touch tools or machines that are not yours.
TURN IT IN / 06 / 06
Pick Your Best and Submit
Back in class, look through everything you shot. Pick your 8 to 10 best photos, the ones with the strongest framing, and upload them to this assignment in Canvas.
